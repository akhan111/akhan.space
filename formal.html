Anthropocentrissus

Acrylic and Semen on Canvas
40" x 30", 2019

Inspired both by Caravaggio and Dalí, this piece equates Narcissus' individual self-infatuation and ensuing self-destruction with that of humanity. However, unlike Caravaggio's symbolic continuous composition, the "reflection" depicted here is broken ‒ a tragic end to the cycle and a warning as we enter the environmental point of no return. The painting is accompanied by the short story "The Myth of Man: Narcissus Retold."

The Great Ape

Acrylic on Canvas
82" x 48", 2017

Inspired by Desmond Morris's book The Naked Ape, I chose to depict one of my favorite humans ‒ myself ‒ in all my natural glory (and then some). This painting was my "Legacy Piece," the final painting I made during my time at the High School for Performing and Visual Arts in Houston, where the work is on permanent display.

Creationism & Communism

Acrylic on Canvas
32" x 48", 2017

In the early 1900s, the Bolsheviks funded attempts to disprove creationism (and thus Christianity) in the name of communism: the Soviet scientist Ilya Ivanovich Ivanov’s experimentation involving the hybridization of humans with other apes was reportedly on track to success before his arrest alongside political disturbance.

Genesis 1:27

Acrylic on Canvas
36" x 48", 2017

“So God created man in His own image.” This piece relies on its own controversy to convey the message. Many cannot accept the glorification of another species to such a high degree. Despite chimpanzees sharing nearly 99% of our DNA, it is seen as offensive or even sacrilegious to place them in a holy context. However, this painting is not meant to denounce the status of religion, but rather to elevate the status of animals.

Primal Desire

Acrylic on Canvas
30" x 40", 2017

The desire that is so private and taboo in modern society links us to the other members of the animal kingdom. Many humans forget that sex is a necessary component of reproduction and evolution, and instead try desperately to mask and hide their natural needs.

Ritual of Refinement

Acrylic on Canvas
36" x 48", 2017

Shaving is a necessary ritual to psychologically isolate us from our ape ancestry. Humans grow hair, though less of it, in many of the same places that other apes grow hair ‒ and the underlying similarities are striking. This piece was inspired by Mongo, a chimpanzee at Twycross Zoo that suffered from alopecia.

These Colors Don't Run

Acrylic on Canvas
54" x 42", 2017

Love him or hate him, one thing is for sure: Trump speaks and acts without restraint. The quality of self-restraint separates us from other species, and, by minimizing this, Trump makes it easier for some to see him as a member of Homonidae (as we all are) rather than as an individual of a greater society. Furthermore, the work investigates how a portrait can blur boundaries across species while still retaining recognizable features of the individual depicted.

Pony

Acrylic on Canvas
42" x 38", 2017

What better way to display our simian musculature than through the age-old ritual of pole dancing? On a heavier note, this piece was inspired by the horrific discovery of an orangutan, named Pony, being exploited as a sex slave in Borneo.

Flanges & Fumes

Acrylic on Canvas
30" x 40", 2017

An orangutan smoking a cigar is ridiculous. So why, then, does this figure seem so familiar? This piece is inspired by the power and authority even another species can possess, but that humans generally only associate with our own kind. Mango

Acrylic on Canvas
24" x 36", 2016

The scenery is drawn from a photo of an orangutan spearfishing ‒ a learned behavior for obtaining food. This is not so different from children I observed climbing trees for ripe mangos in Honduras. In looking at similar behaviors across species, the physical likenesses in both motion and musculature also present themselves more clearly.

The Beggar

Acrylic on Canvas
24" x 36", 2016

Nothing is more tragic than the look of silent desperation in someone's eyes. This can be found in humans, as well as in the creatures that truly can't voice their despair. I saw that look when gazing into this chimpanzee’s concrete cell at the Saigon Zoo and Botanical Gardens. Zoos serve important roles in society, but they must be held to higher standards if humanity is to continue its trajectory of ethical progress.

Beast of Burden

Acrylic on Canvas
24" x 36", 2016

Humans have domesticated wildlife for millennia. Yuval Noah Harari, in his book Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind, argues that in the process, we have been domesticated as well. In evolutionary terms, few species are as successful as the domestic ones, which are constantly being provided for through human labor.

The Missing Link

Acrylic on Canvas
24" x 36", 2016

Countless portraits have been painted of humans as people. This self-portrait instead portrays the human as an animal. This is approached by treating the skin as another artist may treat a coat of fur, by capturing a blank, deer-in-headlights gaze, and by placing the figure in a natural environment devoid of human construction.

Case Study of Homo Sapiens Ssp.

Ink, Paper, Tea, Hair, Ziplock, Thumbtacks on Cork Board
17" x 23", 2016

What would I be like to a stranger, a total stranger, one that is not even familiar with humankind? This idea was explored by making detached observations, as well as often inaccurate assumptions, of my own behavior and appearance. I sought to put myself, a human, on the same level that researchers put other species. In 2017, this piece was displayed in the exhibit "Origins of the Self" at the Contemporary Arts Museum Houston.
